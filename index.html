WHAT A TOP BLOKE!

WHAT A TOP BLOKE!

So - on 28th May 2018 - why was this top bloke struggling to hold back tears while helping choose some plants in a garden centre?

CLUE: It was nothing to do with the flowers on offer.

I had burnt out

I remember a conversation with a friend from 5 or 6 years ago when I said I didn’t understand why some people get so stressed about stuff.

Just don’t worry about it!

Although my tongue was in my cheek at the time, I still think I truly believed that stress was something that affected other people, but not me.

I now realise not only that I am susceptible to stress, but it seems that those positive qualities of hard work, determination and drive have played a huge part in adding to the stress in my life.

The unpleasant experience of holding back tears in a garden centre was the trigger that made me realise I needed to do something about my stress.

There wasn’t one single event that lead to the garden centre experience.

I had been aware of feeling stressed for at least six months and when I reflect on those months, I realise that it was inevitable that I would reach some sort of breaking point.

What is burnout?

There's no officially recognised definition

Common themes from the web:

  • Physical and emotional exhaustion
  • Long term stress
  • Emotionally demanding situations
  • Disillusioned

Maslach Burnout Inventory

https://www.researchgate.net/publication/277816643_The_Maslach_Burnout_Inventory_Manual
  1. Emotional exhaustion
  2. Reduced performance or motivation
  3. Detachment or depersonalisation

The Maslach Burnout Inventory was a piece of research that was published in 1981. It's a multiple choice questionnaire that aims to score a person in these three areas.

MBI was created to assess service workers (e.g. Doctors and Nurses)

Detachment, for me, was the one aspect I couldn't quite relate to expect perhaps for my loss of interest in socialising.

For service workers like doctors and nurses, detachment can mean a dehumanized perception of clients - they are somehow deserving of their troubles.

Mental health can be seen as a big, scary phrase. It can conjure thoughts of old-skool asylums like “One Flew Over the Cuckoo’s Nest” or Arkum, with straight jackets, padded cells and prison-like environment.

Professional help

  • GP
  • BANES Talking Therapies
  • Bristol Wellbeing Therapies

First thing after speaking to Emma - booked the GP.

Talking to family, friends and colleagues is a huge help but speaking to a professional is just as important because they will be trained to lead the conversation in certain directions to help assess your situation and advise suitable treatments.

We all know the NHS is stretched and mental health may not get the attention and funding that it deserves, but there is help available so why not ask for professional help?

The GP recommended making an appointment with BANES Talking Therapies.

2x one-to-one assessments, plus 3x group Stress & Mood Management

Rest

?

Time off is an interesting topic when it comes to work related stress and burnout. Two weeks off might help by removing yourself from the stressful situation and giving your mind a rest, but there’s a danger that going back to work could be even harder if nothing else has changed.

Go back socially (maybe for a lunch) before going back to work? This way you can have any “how are you?” conversations without the added pressure of the work.

GP - Rest options

  1. Get an official sick note and take two weeks off work
  2. Self sign off for one week
  3. Don’t take time off

I was mentally and physically drained so option 3 wasn’t right. We agreed that one week was right for me.

The doctor suggested keeping active by doing some things that I enjoy. I went swimming and running, I read a lot, I went to see an osteopath for my stiff neck, I got a full body massage, and I went to visit my Mum. So it was a pretty therapeutic week and it did me the world of good!

Really coming out

"Giving permission"

"Making yourself vulnerable"

Monzo, Stress: Are We Coping?

So I started a mental-health slack channel

A phrase that came up repeatedly in the Monzo talks was “giving permission”. In this context it means that all it takes is one person to start a public conversation ("make themselves vulnerable"), and this “gives permission” for others to do the same.

So as a male developer in an office full of other male developers, the next move was obvious - I started a mental-health channel on Slack!

Although I was a bit nervous about it, I was also quietly confident that there would be a positive response. I’d already had a couple of brief conversations about other people’s anxiety and depression so I knew there would be people who might like to use a mental-health channel.

The response was really positive and the channel has been used to share recommendations for podcasts, articles and books. There was also a kind of #metoo effect where people have shared the fact that they’ve experienced problems in the past and come through them.

Mood Diary

Because developers love logging!!!

  • Daylio (iOS/Android)
  • Moodnotes (iOS, one of the speakers in the Monzo talks)
  • Pad & paper

First step that developers take when debugging?

More logging!!!

Daylio has a focus on making really quick entries with no need to type anything - and is useful for correlation.

Moodnotes uses techniques from Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT)

Anything that enables reflection on your past thoughts or moods can be helpful.

I've been making at least two entries a day - 9:00 AM/PM - or when I've noticed my mood changes.

I find it helps to keep yourself aware of how you're actually feeling.

Looking back over the entries for the past few weeks can be a really interesting exercise, especially when you start to correlate activities with moods.

Look after yourself

Comparing yourself to others is one of the most unhelpful things you can do in life.

Nick Soper

I know my mental health issues haven’t got to an extremely serious point when compared to other people - I’ve never had a panic attack, I can leave the house on my own, I’ve never contemplated suicide - but that does not mean that my own problems aren’t worthy of my attention.

I remember Scroobius Pip making this point in one of his podcasts and it really stuck with me.

It might be true that there is always someone worse off than yourself, but if you broke your toe you probably wouldn’t avoid getting it treated just because there are people in the world who have broken their back!

We shouldn’t consider issues with the mind to be any less worthy of treatment than issues with the body.

Your problems are worthy of your attention!
